AdTech 1: What Makes an Ad Clickable?

Decomposing click-through rate into viewability and quality

2026-02-12

What makes a user click an ad? In digital advertising, two things need to happen: the user must see the ad, and the ad must be compelling enough to act on. In other words, clickability decomposes into two independent factors:

  • Viewability ($V$) — how likely is it that the ad is actually seen?
  • Quality ($Q$) — given the ad is seen, how relevant and persuasive is it?

This post formalizes that intuition. Starting from first principles, we derive a decomposition of the observed click-through rate:

$$P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}) = Q \cdot \bigl[\, V + r \cdot (1 - V) \,\bigr]$$

where $r \in (0,1)$ is a small false-click factor accounting for accidental or bot clicks on unseen ads. The derivation makes precise what advertisers mean when they say an ad “performs well” — and where the levers actually are.

Setup

In this framework, we consider three random variables and one latent event:

  • click — the user clicks on the ad
  • context — the publication context (site, position, device, etc.)
  • view — the ad is actually seen by the user (latent)

The quantity we often observe in logs is $P(\text{click} \mid \text{context})$, but what we really care about is the true click rate conditioned on the ad being viewed: $P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}, \text{view})$.

Derivation

Start from the joint probability and marginalize over the latent view event:

$$P(\text{click}, \text{context}) = P(\text{click},\text{context}, \text{view}) + P(\text{click},\text{context}, \neg\text{view})$$

Applying the Bayes' rule to each term:

$$P(\text{click}, \text{context}) = P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}, \text{view})\, P(\text{context}, \text{view}) + P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}, \neg\text{view})\, P(\text{context}, \neg\text{view})$$

Dividing both sides by $P(\text{context})$:

$$P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}) = P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}, \text{view})\, P(\text{view} \mid \text{context}) + P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}, \neg\text{view})\, P(\neg\text{view} \mid \text{context})$$

Viewability and the false-click factor

We introduce two quantities to simplify the expression:

Viewability index. The probability that a displayed ad is actually viewed in a given context:

$$V = P(\text{view} \mid \text{context})$$

False-click factor. A scalar $r \in [0, 1]$ that captures the ratio of click probability when the ad is not viewed versus when it is:

$$P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}, \neg\text{view}) = r \cdot P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}, \text{view})$$

False clicks can be attributed to multiple factors such as accidental clicks, bots. When $r = 0$, users never click ads they haven't seen (the ideal case). When $r > 0$, there is some residual click rate from accidental clicks, bot traffic, or misattributed events. In practise we can expect $r$ to be negligible overall.

Substituting into the boxed equation and writing

$$Q = P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}, \text{view})$$

leading to the final identity

$$\boxed{P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}) = Q \cdot \bigl[\, V + r \cdot (1 - V) \,\bigr]}$$

The term in brackets is a viewability correction factor. It tells us how much of the true CTR is preserved in the observed CTR, as a function of viewability $V$ and false-click rate $r$.

Interpretation

  • When $V = 1$ (every displayed ad is viewed), the correction factor equals $1$ regardless of $r$, and the observed CTR matches the true CTR.
  • When $V = 0$ (no ad is viewed), the correction factor equals $r$ — the observed CTR is purely from false clicks.
  • For intermediate values, the correction factor interpolates linearly between $r$ and $1$.

Conclusion: two levers for clickability

The decomposition $\text{CTR} = Q \cdot [V + r(1-V)]$ separates clickability into two fundamentally different problems that require different solutions:

Viewability $V$ is a supply-side quantity. It depends on where the ad is placed (above or below the fold), on which device it is rendered, and on user scroll behaviour. Improving $V$ is about better inventory selection, page layout, and ad placement strategy. It has nothing to do with the ad creative itself.

Quality $Q$ is a demand-side quantity. It captures how relevant the ad is to the user, how persuasive the creative is, and whether the landing page delivers on the promise. Improving $Q$ is about better targeting, creative optimization, and audience understanding. It has nothing to do with the placement.

Conflating the two leads to poor decisions. An ad with high $Q$ on a low-$V$ placement will show a low observed CTR — not because the ad is bad, but because nobody sees it. Conversely, a mediocre ad on a premium above-the-fold spot ($V \approx 1$) will expose its low $Q$ directly.

AdTech 2: What Makes an Ad Viewable?

Decomposing viewability into structural and attentional factors

2026-02-17 · Work in progress

In the previous post, we derived $P(\text{click} \mid \text{context}) = Q \cdot [V + r(1-V)]$, where $V = P(\text{view} \mid \text{context})$ is the viewability score. We treated $V$ as a single number. This post asks: what determines $V$?

1. Statistical Framework

An ad being viewable (meeting the IAB/MRC standard) requires two things: the ad must enter the user’s viewport, and the user must actually notice it. Let $s$ denote a specific ad spot/placement on a page. We marginalize over the latent event “in-viewport”:

$$P(\text{view} \mid s) = P(\text{view} \mid \text{in-viewport}, s) \cdot P(\text{in-viewport} \mid s) + P(\text{view} \mid \neg\text{in-viewport}, s) \cdot P(\neg\text{in-viewport} \mid s)$$

If viewing requires the ad to be in viewport — you cannot register a viewable impression of an ad that never appeared on screen — then $P(\text{view} \mid \neg\text{in-viewport}, s) = 0$ and:

$$\boxed{P(\text{view} \mid s) = P(\text{view} \mid \text{in-viewport}, s) \cdot P(\text{in-viewport} \mid s)}$$

This gives us two distinct factors:

  • $P(\text{in-viewport} \mid s)$ — the structural scroll/fold probability. Does the user scroll far enough for the ad spot to enter the viewport?
  • $P(\text{view} \mid \text{in-viewport}, s)$ — the attentional capture. Given the spot is on screen, does the ad grab the user’s attention?

A saliency model estimates the latter, not the former. If scroll behaviour is not accounted for, you will systematically overestimate $V$ for below-the-fold spots.

2. Empirical properties driving viewability

Before building a parametric model, it helps to identify the concrete factors that influence viewability. Each maps onto one of the two terms in our decomposition:

FactorEffectImpacts
Ad spot size Larger spots occupy more visual real estate, making them harder to ignore $P(\text{view} \mid \text{in-viewport}, s)$ ↑
Scroll depth required Spots further down the page are reached by fewer users $P(\text{in-viewport} \mid s)$ ↓
Intrinsic eye-catchiness Contrast, motion, colour — how much the creative grabs attention $P(\text{view} \mid \text{in-viewport}, s)$ ↑
Page loading delay Slow-loading ads may never render before the user scrolls past $P(\text{in-viewport} \mid s)$ ↓

Some of these factors are structural (scroll depth, page speed) and determined by the publisher’s page layout and infrastructure. Others are creative (size, eye-catchiness) and under the advertiser’s control.

From an optimization standpoint, the actionable lever for the advertiser is the attentional term $P(\text{view} \mid \text{in-viewport}, s)$. In a typical page, an ad competes with other ads and editorial content for the user’s attention. The more attention-grabbing the creative, the higher the probability that the ad is actually noticed once it enters the viewport. This is why creative design matters for viewability — not just for persuasion.

To ground the discussion, here are viewability benchmarks from Pixalate’s Q3 2025 report, based on 39 billion global open programmatic impressions.

A few observations: viewability hovers around 60–65% globally, meaning roughly one in three displayed ads is never actually viewed. Mobile in-app tends to outperform web in most markets (likely due to full-screen or interstitial formats). The variance across countries is substantial — the Netherlands achieves 72% on mobile web while France sits at 49% — reflecting differences in page layouts, ad density, and user behaviour.
